# Production Crew

The Piganelli Lab is a collaborative research group at Indiana University School of Medicine studying type 1 diabetes, redox immunology, T cell metabolism, beta-cell stress, and early immune biomarkers.

<p><a class="button" href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/jon-piganelli-076505212/" target="_blank">LinkedIn Profile</a></p>

<p>Dr. Jon D. Piganelli is a Professor of Medicine in the Department of Endocrinology at Indiana University School of Medicine, where he leads a research program focused on the immune-mediated pathogenesis of type 1 diabetes. His work centers on T cell–mediated beta-cell destruction, redox signaling, oxidative stress, immunometabolism, beta-cell stress, and therapeutic strategies designed to slow, prevent, or reverse disease progression. His scientific background spans immunology, microbiology, biochemistry, metabolism, endocrinology, oxidative stress, and free radical biology, making him a widely sought source of advice across collaborative research spaces. IU notes that he joined IU School of Medicine in 2023 after overseeing a well-funded research program at the University of Pittsburgh for 22 years, and that his work has been supported by the NIH, American Diabetes Association, and JDRF/Breakthrough T1D.<p></p>With more than 35 years of scientific training, Dr. Piganelli has contributed significantly meaningful research to the fields of type 1 diabetes, autoimmunity, redox biology, immunometabolism, and beta-cell stress. His lab reflects both the depth of his scientific expertise and his commitment to building an inclusive, collaborative environment where trainees, staff, and scientists from different backgrounds can grow together. He has mentored postdoctoral fellows, graduate students, medical fellows, undergraduate students, and national and international trainees throughout his career.<p></p>Dr. Piganelli can almost always be found in his office listening to golden oldies, classic rock, and nostalgic throwbacks while zoning in on the grant writing that keeps the lab’s research moving forward. On his off time, Dr. Piganelli loves to research new discoveries in astrophysics research and excitedly sharing his findings with us. He is also known for assigning nicknames to almost everyone and somehow never forgetting them. His generosity with his time, his instinct to put others first, and his commitment to bringing people into science are central to the culture of the Piganelli Lab.
</p>

<h2 class="section-title">Lab Members</h2>

<div class="people-card">

  <img class="people-headshot" src="assets/images/Roy.png" alt="Saptarshi Roy headshot" style="object-position: center 35%;">

  <div class="people-info">
    <h3>Saptarshi Roy, PhD</h3>

<p><strong>Assistant Professor of Medicine</strong></p>
<p><strong>Department of Endocrinology / Department of Medicine</strong></p>
<p><strong>Indiana University School of Medicine</strong></p>
<p><strong>Email:</strong> <a href="mailto:roysapt@iu.edu">roysapt@iu.edu</a></p>
<p><a class="button" href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/saptarshi-roy-195515180/" target="_blank">LinkedIn Profile</a></p>

<p>Dr. Saptarshi Roy is an Assistant Research Professor of Medicine in the Piganelli Lab at Indiana University School of Medicine. His work contributes to the lab’s studies of immune regulation, beta-cell stress, redox biology, and type 1 diabetes pathogenesis. His training bridges parasitology, host–pathogen biology, immunology, autoimmune disease, and translational biomarker research. He began his graduate education and scientific training at Visva-Bharati University, while continuing postdoctoral training at the University of Texas at Tyler and the University of Pittsburgh. Indiana University, and his current role as Assistant Research Professor of Medicine. Dr. Saptarshi Roy spent his final years as a Post-Doc at Indiana University, and was deservedly awarded his current role as Assistant Research Professor of Medicine by IUI.<p><p>In the Piganelli Lab, Dr. Roy plays a central role in studying the early immune events that may drive type 1 diabetes before clinical disease is fully established and investigating other related immunometabolism changes throughout these metabolic diseases. His current work focuses on autoimmune diabetes, early biomarker development, viral infections associated with beta-cell stress, neoantigen formation, and beta-cell autoimmunity. His recent work on soluble LAG-3 investigates whether this marker can identify an early T cell activation window in type 1 diabetes that may appear before traditional islet autoantibodies.<p></p>Beyond his scientific role, Dr. Roy is known throughout the unit as one of the most admired and dependable experimentalists. He has a rare ability to give generously to others without letting his own work fall behind. Whether staying from early morning until midnight to finish time-sensitive experiments or coming in on weekends to help with studies that require careful timing, he brings a level of dedication, patience, and technical skill that strengthens the entire lab. His well-earned transition from postdoctoral fellow to Assistant Research Professor reflects years of hard work, consistency, and the respect he has built across the university. When he is not in the lab, Dr. Roy is spending time with his wife and their baby girl, Tanishka. He also has a sharp eye for design and a refined sense of color, which shows up in everything from his fashion and gifts to his data analysis, figures, and presentations. A fun fact about Roy is that he is vegetarian, and because of that, he is always a safe person to ask about different cuisines, snacks, and foods he loves from India.
</p>
