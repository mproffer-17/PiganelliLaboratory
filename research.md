# Research

The Piganelli Lab studies how immune cells, beta cells, oxidative stress, metabolism, and antigen-specific T cell responses interact to drive type 1 diabetes.

Our research program combines mouse models, human samples, flow cytometry, antigen-specific tetramers, molecular assays, and translational immunology approaches to understand how autoimmune diabetes begins, progresses, and may be interrupted.

---

<h2 class="section-title">Research Productions</h2>

<p class="section-intro">
  Each research theme represents one part of the larger story of autoimmune diabetes: how beta-cell stress begins, how immune cells recognize self-antigens, how T cells become activated and metabolically reprogrammed, and how these events may be detected or therapeutically targeted.
</p>

<section class="production-grid">

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production I</p>
    <h3>Redox Immunology</h3>
    <p>
      We investigate how oxidative stress and redox signaling shape immune activation, inflammatory responses, and autoimmune diabetes progression.
    </p>
    <p>
      This work focuses on how reactive oxygen species and redox-dependent pathways influence T cell function, immune regulation, and tissue-specific inflammation.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production II</p>
    <h3>T Cell Metabolism</h3>
    <p>
      Activated autoreactive T cells change how they use energy. We study how metabolic reprogramming supports T cell activation, proliferation, survival, and tissue infiltration.
    </p>
    <p>
      These studies help define how immunometabolic pathways may be targeted to reduce pathogenic autoimmune responses.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production III</p>
    <h3>Beta-Cell Stress</h3>
    <p>
      We examine how stressed beta cells may contribute to immune recognition, antigen presentation, and type 1 diabetes progression.
    </p>
    <p>
      This includes interest in endoplasmic reticulum stress, beta-cell dysfunction, and the generation of modified or stress-associated antigens.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production IV</p>
    <h3>Early Biomarkers</h3>
    <p>
      We work to identify immune signals that may detect autoimmune activation before overt clinical diabetes develops.
    </p>
    <p>
      These studies include early immune activation markers, antigen-specific T cell responses, beta-cell stress-associated readouts, and soluble immune biomarkers.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production V</p>
    <h3>Antigen-Specific T Cells</h3>
    <p>
      We use antigen-specific approaches, including tetramer-based analyses, to study autoreactive T cell responses in type 1 diabetes.
    </p>
    <p>
      This allows us to follow how self-reactive T cells emerge, expand, diversify, and contribute to autoimmune beta-cell destruction.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="production-card">
    <p class="production-number">Production VI</p>
    <h3>Translational Models</h3>
    <p>
      We use preclinical and translational systems to connect mechanistic immune findings with disease progression, biomarker discovery, and potential therapeutic strategies.
    </p>
    <p>
      These approaches help bridge basic immunology, animal models, and human type 1 diabetes research.
    </p>
  </div>

</section>

<h2 class="section-title">Approaches</h2>

<p class="section-intro">
  The Piganelli Lab uses complementary mouse, cellular, molecular, and translational approaches to study how autoimmune diabetes begins, progresses, and may be interrupted.
</p>

<div class="card-grid">

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Mouse Models of Autoimmune Diabetes</h3>
    <p>
      We use autoimmune diabetes mouse models to study disease initiation, immune activation, beta-cell stress, antigen-specific T cell responses, and therapeutic strategies that may alter disease progression.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Adoptive Transfer Systems</h3>
    <p>
      Adoptive transfer systems allow us to examine how autoreactive T cells initiate disease, expand over time, traffic into tissues, and contribute to beta-cell destruction.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Flow Cytometry and Immune Phenotyping</h3>
    <p>
      We use multiparameter flow cytometry to define immune cell populations, activation states, metabolic profiles, and antigen-specific T cell responses across blood, spleen, pancreas, and other tissues.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Antigen-Specific Tetramer Analysis</h3>
    <p>
      We use peptide-MHC tetramers to identify and track rare autoreactive T cells that recognize beta-cell antigens during type 1 diabetes progression.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Redox Biology</h3>
    <p>
      We investigate how redox signaling, oxidative stress, and reactive oxygen species influence immune activation, beta-cell stress, and autoimmune diabetes progression.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>T Cell Immunometabolism</h3>
    <p>
      We study how immune cells change their metabolic programs during activation and autoimmune disease, with an emphasis on pathways that support autoreactive T cell survival, expansion, and effector function.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Beta-Cell Stress and Tissue Analysis</h3>
    <p>
      We examine how beta-cell stress, tissue inflammation, and pancreatic immune infiltration contribute to autoimmune recognition and disease progression.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Soluble Biomarker and Molecular Assays</h3>
    <p>
      We measure soluble and molecular biomarkers that may reflect early immune activation, beta-cell stress, and disease progression before overt clinical diabetes.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Human Translational Studies</h3>
    <p>
      We integrate human samples and clinical cohort studies to evaluate whether mechanisms observed in model systems are relevant to human type 1 diabetes risk, progression, and immune activation.
    </p>
  </div>

  <div class="card">
    <h3>Preclinical Therapeutic Studies</h3>
    <p>
      We test therapeutic strategies that target immune activation, redox balance, beta-cell stress, or T cell metabolism to determine whether autoimmune diabetes progression can be delayed or altered.
    </p>
  </div>

</div>
